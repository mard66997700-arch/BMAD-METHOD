# ADR-004 — Translation engine selection per language corridor

## Context

The product must support ≥20 cloud-served language pairs (PRD FR-4). No single MT vendor wins across all corridors:

- **DeepL** leads on European pairs (EN↔DE/FR/ES/IT/NL/PL) and has strong EN↔ZH/JA but covers only 32 languages.
- **Google Cloud Translation** has the broadest language coverage (130+) and a strong baseline but is not always the best on European corridors.
- **OpenAI GPT-4o-mini** can produce more idiomatic output and preserve register if prompted carefully, at higher per-token cost.
- **Microsoft Translator**, **Yandex**, **Naver Papago**, **DeepL** all have language-specific strengths.

PRD FR-4 also names languages that DeepL does not cover (HI, BN, TH, VI, ID, TR — partially), forcing a multi-vendor strategy regardless of preference.

## Decision

**Adopt a corridor-keyed routing policy table** that maps `(source language, target language) → vendor` independently for each layer (STT, MT, TTS). Engine selection is **data-driven**: the policy table is fetched from a remote config endpoint at app start and refreshed daily, so we can shift traffic between vendors without an app release.

**Default policy at v1 launch:**

| Corridor (MT) | Primary | Fallback (on primary failure) |
|--------------|---------|-------------------------------|
| EN ↔ {DE, FR, ES, IT, NL, PL, PT, RU, JA, ZH} | DeepL | Google |
| EN ↔ {KO} | Naver Papago | Google |
| EN ↔ {VI, TH, ID, HI, BN, AR, TR} | Google | NLLB-200 (server-side) |
| Any pair where DeepL lacks support | Google | NLLB-200 |
| Pro tier "context-aware" mode | GPT-4o-mini with rolling context window | DeepL/Google |
| Offline (any pair) | NLLB-200 600M (on-device) | none |

A separate but parallel policy applies to STT and TTS (architecture §3.4).

## Rationale

- **Quality is corridor-specific.** Forcing one vendor everywhere costs quality somewhere.
- **Vendor risk diversification.** Single-vendor dependence is operationally risky (API outages, pricing changes, ToS changes).
- **Cost optimization.** DeepL is more expensive than Google by ~25%; using it only where it wins keeps blended cost in the optimization-zone.
- **Rapid response to user feedback.** Remote config means a quality complaint about VI translation can be mitigated within hours by shifting to a different vendor.

## Consequences

**Positive**

- Best-in-corridor quality.
- Resilience to single-vendor outages (a corridor degrades to fallback rather than the whole product going down).
- Cost efficiency.

**Negative**

- Higher integration surface — we maintain SDKs/clients for 4–5 vendors.
- Quality is harder to reason about ("which vendor was used on my translation?") — mitigated by the engine-transparency sheet in UX §3.2.
- Vendor consolidation in the industry could reduce options (e.g. acquisitions); the abstraction layer cushions this.

## Alternatives Considered

- **Single-vendor (Google).** Easiest to operate but accepts a quality discount on European corridors (DeepL leads). Rejected.
- **Single-vendor (DeepL).** Best European quality but unacceptable language coverage for FR-4. Rejected.
- **GPT-4o-mini for everything.** Tempting (uniform quality, streaming tokens) but ~5× more expensive at v1 scale. Rejected for free tier; offered as a Pro "context-aware" mode.
- **Static routing baked into the app.** Rejected because shifting routing requires an app update — too slow.

## Revisit Triggers

- A new vendor becomes a clear leader across most corridors (e.g. NLLB or a new open model leapfrogs the field).
- DeepL adds the FR-4 long-tail languages and matches Google's coverage.
- Per-vendor costs change materially.
